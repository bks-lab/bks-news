## Story of the Week

### DeepSeek's mHC: China's Efficiency Innovation in AI Training

In early January 2026, Chinese AI startup DeepSeek published a technical paper that could rethink the foundation of AI training [[1]](#source-1). The method is called "Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections" (mHC) and promises to train larger models at significantly lower costs.

The innovation addresses a fundamental problem of current training architectures: increasing memory costs for large models [[1]](#source-1). DeepSeek's solution adds constraints that ensure both compute and cost efficiency without compromising training stability. Tests ran on models ranging from 3 to 27 billion parameters [[1]](#source-1).

Particularly noteworthy: DeepSeek founder Liang Wenfeng personally uploaded the paper to arXiv – a signal that this represents a central strategic direction [[1]](#source-1). Based on DeepSeek's publication patterns, observers expect a new flagship model before the Chinese Spring Festival in mid-February.

**Assessment:** According to the paper, the method brings 6-7% training overhead but improves stability at large scale [[1]](#source-1). Independent validation of the results is still pending – the paper is only a few days old.

**Bottom Line:** While the US relies on chip advantage, China is gaining ground through efficiency innovations – an arms race that could fundamentally change the AI landscape in 2026.

### California SB 53: The First US Frontier AI Regulation

On January 1, 2026, California's Senate Bill 53 went into effect – the first US regulation specifically for Frontier AI Models [[4]](#source-4). The law defines Frontier Models as foundation models trained with more than 10^26 computational operations [[4]](#source-4). Approximately 5-8 companies are affected: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, and Microsoft [[4]](#source-4).

The core requirements: Large Frontier Developers (annual revenue >$500M) must annually publish frameworks documenting how they identify and mitigate catastrophic risks [[4]](#source-4). All Frontier Developers must publish transparency reports before deployment [[4]](#source-4). Critical safety incidents must be reported to California's Office of Emergency Services within 15 days – within 24 hours in case of imminent danger of death [[4]](#source-4).

Penalties for violations: Up to $1 million per violation [[4]](#source-4). Governor Newsom positioned the law as a blueprint for other US states. For comparison: New York's pending RAISE Act provides for penalties of $10-30 million [[4]](#source-4).

### MCP Becomes Standard: Adoption Grows – Security Questions Remain

Since Anthropic's introduction of the Model Context Protocol in November 2024, adoption has been growing rapidly [[5]](#source-5). Native integration has already occurred in Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, Cursor, and Visual Studio Code. Early adopters like Block and Apollo have integrated MCP into their systems [[5]](#source-5).

The problem MCP solves: AI systems had no direct access to enterprise data – each integration had to be built individually, legacy systems remained isolated [[5]](#source-5). MCP replaces fragmented integrations with a universal standard, comparable to USB-C for hardware.

**Important Context:** While MCP is promising, the security community warns of still immature security aspects: Prompt Injection Vulnerabilities, Mutable Tool Definitions, and Token Theft Risks are documented concerns. The technology is powerful, but the security ecosystem is still in early phases.

Google has already launched fully managed MCP servers for Maps, BigQuery, Compute Engine, and Kubernetes Engine [[5]](#source-5).
